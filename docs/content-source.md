### Sigma (product name: Oshin)
**A distribution-first equity-research terminal.**

**The problem.** Most research tools give you a consensus mean and a single price target. That throws away the most useful information an analyst has: the shape of their own conviction across outcomes, and how it disagrees with what the market is already pricing. And nobody scores whether your probabilistic calls were any good after the fact.

**What I built.** Sigma takes the live options chain, derives the market's full risk-neutral probability distribution from it, overlays my own Bull/Base/Bear distribution on the same axis, and shades the gap between the two as edge. Every view ends in three things: an expected value, a strategy comparison (long stock vs ATM call vs OTM call vs ATM put, each priced under your density against its market cost), and a half-Kelly position size. Then it grades you. The calibration view tracks your probabilistic calls over time with a Brier score and a reliability diagram, the same way a serious poker player measures process over outcome.

**The wedge.** The options-implied-distribution math already exists in trading tools, so I never claim it's novel. The genuinely open space is two-fold: no fundamental-research product reconciles an analyst's catalyst distribution against the options market's implied distribution on one axis, and calibration of your own forecasts lives only in prediction markets, never in an equity-research UI. The detail I'm proudest of is the honesty: the implied density is labeled risk-neutral (Q), not real-world (P). Sigma surfaces the volatility risk premium explicitly and offers a transparent Q-to-P drift adjustment instead of mislabeling Q as P, which is the red-flag error that naive "implied probability" tools make.

**The stack.** Next.js 16, React 19, TypeScript, Tailwind v4, Radix, d3, Vitest. The quant lives in pure, dependency-free TypeScript so it can be defended line by line: Black-Scholes, the Breeden-Litzenberger risk-neutral density pipeline, expected move, EV, Kelly, VRP, and calibration, all unit-tested. The headline test feeds the pipeline a chain priced off a flat IV surface and asserts it recovers the closed-form lognormal density, which is the correctness contract. Data is hybrid: snapshot-first so a demo can't break, with a live mode that pulls real option chains. The AI scenario engine is deliberately anchored on the options-implied base rate and made to justify any deviation, never a bare model probability.

**Status.** Built as a focused, defensible artifact. It's the most quant-heavy thing I've made, and it's downstream of my equity-research seat at Manatuck Hill and my research-distribution work, reframed around distributions and calibration instead of calls.

### Galactic Signals (galacticsignals.com)
**A Discord-first monitoring platform that delivers AI-powered, cross-asset alerts straight into communities.** The thesis: in a few years everyone will have a personal AI agent, so the durable moat won't be the AI itself, it'll be the data feeding it.

**The problem.** Cross-asset monitoring is fragmented. If you want stocks, crypto, sports betting, trading cards, real estate, macro, and news in one place, delivered where your community already lives, no single tool covers it.

**What I built (with my co-founder).** Galactic is a real two-person venture. I'm the non-technical founder, product owner, and distribution lead; my longtime technical co-founder owns the engineering. The product unifies cross-asset monitoring with multi-channel delivery and a creator-driven marketplace. The smartest decision was killing the Discord bot in favor of pure webhooks. A user logs in, browses a store of data categories, activates what they want behind Stripe, pastes a Discord webhook URL into their dashboard, and starts receiving branded embed alerts in about thirty seconds. No bot install, no OAuth dance, and an architecture that extends cleanly to Telegram, Slack, or email.

**The stack.** Two repos. The web app (billing, store, admin) is Next.js 16 / React 19 / TypeScript, Tailwind v4 + shadcn/ui, Prisma against PostgreSQL, NextAuth with Google and Discord OAuth, Stripe, Resend, Sentry. The data engine is a separate fleet of around 79 async Python workers, one per feed: top gainers, congressional trades, ETF flows, earnings revisions, crypto funding rates, and dozens more. Each worker subclasses a common base that owns its polling loop, market-hours gating, and a per-worker circuit breaker, plus heartbeat recording. A delivery engine renders the branded embeds and fans them out with a token-bucket rate limiter tuned to Discord's limits. The whole thing is self-hosted via Docker Compose behind nginx, with GitHub Actions auto-deploying on push to main. That's a deliberate departure from my usual Vercel + Supabase stack, and it gave me real production-resilience reps.

**Status.** Real, ongoing venture, currently pre-launch. It's the latest evolution of the longest-running thread in my career, the Discord-native data business that began as Mocean. Mocean to Galactic is one continuous arc: each rebrand generalized the same idea, from NFT and crypto alpha to any monitorable data stream.

### Observly
**A two-sided marketplace for clinical shadowing.** Observly connects pre-med students with practicing physicians for shadowing, mentorship, and verified hour-tracking. It's an early-stage healthcare venture with its own company, not a solo side project.

**The problem.** Pre-meds need shadowing hours and physician mentorship, and there's no clean way to find a willing doctor, book the time, and then prove the hours later when medical-school applications are due. The existing options are expensive pay-to-shadow services with a real stigma problem.

**What I built (as part of the founding team).** Doctors post opportunities and open availability; students browse, apply, and book; both sides confirm completion so logged hours become verified hours. On top of the booking layer sits LinkedIn-style connections and real-time messaging. There are two role-scoped experiences, a Doctor dashboard and a Student dashboard. The strategy is the interesting part: we chose to launch free, to dodge the pay-to-shadow stigma and to qualify for grant and non-profit framing, then move to a LinkedIn-style freemium model, pay to win, not pay to play.

**The stack.** Next.js 16 (App Router, Turbopack), React 19, strict TypeScript. Supabase for auth, Postgres, and Realtime messaging. Tailwind 4, Radix in the shadcn pattern, Recharts, react-hook-form. It's the most database-heavy project I've worked on: schema lives in fifteen sequential SQL migrations with auto-generated TypeScript types and a drift-check that fails the build if the committed types diverge from the migrations. Authorization is enforced in Postgres with Row-Level Security, not just in the app, down to rate limits in RLS. Hour-tracking is dual-confirmation by design so verified hours actually mean something.

**Status.** Active venture, team build. Sits alongside BrachyClip in the healthcare lane of my work.

### BriefBridge
**Turn chaotic case files into findable records, in seconds instead of hours.** BriefBridge is an AI tool for insurance-litigation law firms that ingests one giant compiled case-file PDF and automatically splits it into individual documents, classifies each one, and files it under the case.

**The problem.** Insurance-defence firms receive case material as a single PDF that staples together hundreds of pages: medical reports, court filings, insurer letters, wage records, witness statements, in no particular order. Splitting and filing that by hand is hours of paralegal grunt work, and finding any one record later is its own slog.

**What I built.** Upload the compiled PDF, the ML service detects document boundaries, splits the PDF, classifies each resulting document into a category, and files it. The web app then gives the firm a case workspace with per-document review, full-text search across the case, and a timeline view.

**The stack.** A Turborepo monorepo with two apps and a shared types package. The web app is Next.js (App Router) with Supabase for auth and storage, Prisma over Postgres, Tailwind + shadcn/ui, Zustand. The document-processing brain is a Python FastAPI service with five stages: an extractor (PyMuPDF plus Tesseract OCR for scanned pages), a boundary detector (text-signal heuristics combined with a Claude call), a splitter, a Claude-powered classifier, and an embedder. The data model is multi-tenant by organization with audit logging on everything. A shared package holds the literal-union status enums that keep the TypeScript and Python sides from drifting across the service seam, a discipline I learned the hard way on multi-service builds.

**Status.** Active build. The cleanest example of a pattern that runs through a lot of my AI work: ingest messy documents, use Claude to classify and structure them, and give a professional a workspace that turns hours into seconds.
